How to Avoid Another All-Nighter






We've all been there before.

You just bombed an exam worse than Pearl Harbor. You tried to shovel two months’ worth of biology into your brain in one night because procrastination got the best of you. But the worst is over. You’ve told yourself you’re going to study a lot harder for the next exam. You’re going to hit the library every day; you’ll even bring a sleeping bag if you have to. You’re going to get to know your professor so well that even NSA would be jealous. You’ve promised yourself a glorious comeback. The next exam will get the wrath of nerds.

And then several months later that next exam is scheduled at 9:00 a.m. and you’re hunched over your computer at 5:00 a.m. trying to cram two months’ worth of biology…

I know all about this cycle because this sums up my sophomore year at SFA. We’ve all started off with great intentions of making great grades only to see our ambitions crushed like the snooze buttons on our alarm clocks. Lucky for me, I went on to make the President’s List my junior year because I learned how to set goals correctly.

Here’s how:

My old boss calls this “Pre-Think.” It’s the process of identifying your goals and writing down ever reason you’ll fail. That’s right, fail. Because when we set goals we tend to be overly optimistic without remembering that we’re human.

Here’s an example:

You want to spend the whole day writing a research paper?

Well, what will you do when your friends invite you to workout at the Rec Center? What if you get hungry? What about when you get tired? What about all those important social media websites that need updates?

Writing down every reason you might hiccup allows you to creatively eliminate obstacles that may stand in your way. For instance, if you’re a Facebook junkie use the Anti-Social app to block that site during certain timeframes. Consider taking a jog to wake yourself up before long hours of cramming. Maybe even pack your gym clothes before class. Xbox in your room? Ask a friend or desk assistant to hold on to it for that day. And make sure to get that paper done a day early so you can have it edited by the AARC. Boom. Pre-think.

Next example:

You want to make the Dean’s List?

What if you bomb the first exam? How many classes can you afford to miss? Personally, if I failed an exam I figured out numerically what’s the minimum grade I could make on each assignment to reach my goal. And don’t skip classes. Put your alarm clock across the hall if you need to. Or download the Game Up Alarm Clock app that forces you to solve puzzles before it shuts off.

So before you go off setting lofty goals, identify your weaknesses. Good luck this semester.

Written by Piero Pretto, SFA Graduate
