Writing A Research Paper In Half The Time





“The sooner you dispel the notion that writing is the most important part of paper writing, the easier it will become for you to reap the benefits of the straight-A approach." - Cal Newport


In the book “How To Become A Straight-A Student” by Cal Newport, the author lays out a game plan for cranking out research papers. Here’s the cliffnotes in 3 easy steps:

1) Shift through existing arguments

2) Form your own argument

3) Communicate your argument clearly

According to Newport, most students try to do all three steps at the same time.

He states - “They sit down at their computer, stack up some sources, and then begin writing with only a vague idea of where they’re headed. Whenever their argument stalls, they flip through their sources until they find an interesting quote, they insert this quote into their document, and then let their argument continue in this new direction, until it stalls once again – at which point, it’s back to the sources. This cycle of research/think/write continues slowly for hours as the paper is constructed, one painful paragraph at a time.”

Sound familiar?

The “straight-A approach,” as Cal calls it, is to research for a thesis, construct an argument, and then let the paper write itself with all the information you have in front you.

Here's 5 tips from the book to help implement this approach:

1) Conducting a thesis-hunting expedition

Start with general sources and then follow references to find more targeted sources.

2) Crafting a Powerful Story

Start with a topic-level outline to fully describe your argument. Then write.

3) Consult your expert panel

Get some opinions on the organization of your argument and your support from classmates and friends who are familiar with the general area of study.

4) "The Out Loud Pass"

Carefully read out loud a printed copy of your paper, marking any awkward passages or unclear explanations

5) Make a photocopy or printout of all relevant material

Writing a research paper can get messy and flipping through multiple tabs, trying to remember where the “good” quotes are can be time-consuming. If you find a good source, print it out. Have everything you need laid out in front of you or in a folder before you start writing.
